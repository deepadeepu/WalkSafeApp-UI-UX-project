# 🚶 WalkSafe — Walking Route Safety App
### UX Research Case Study

A user research investigation into pedestrian safety perceptions, route decision-making, and the appetite for community-powered safety tools in local communities.

---

## 📋 Project Overview

This case study documents secondary survey research conducted to understand the lived experiences of everyday walkers — what makes them feel unsafe, how they currently navigate risk, and whether a community-powered walking safety app could meaningfully address their needs.

The findings directly informed a set of prioritised UX recommendations and lo-fi app screen concepts for **WalkSafe**, a proposed mobile application for safer community walking routes.

---

## 📊 Research Summary

| | |
|---|---|
| **Method** | Secondary online survey |
| **Respondents** | 20 participants |
| **Questions** | 12 (single-select, multi-select, open-ended) |
| **Period** | September 2025 |
| **Age Range** | 16–60+ |

---

## 🔑 Key Findings

- **80%** of respondents felt unsafe or unsure while walking
- **55%** would *definitely* use a safety route app; 35% would consider it
- **75%** named poor lighting as their top safety concern
- **50%** rely solely on familiar routes, limiting their freedom of movement
- **95%** are willing to contribute safety reports to help other walkers
- **Real-time alerts** and **safety route ratings** were the most desired features

---

## 👥 User Personas

Three archetypes were synthesised from the survey data:

**Priya — The Daily Commuter**
Walks to work multiple times a day. Needs pre-trip safety intelligence. Has experienced harassment and relies on familiarity as a safety proxy.

**Marcus — The Fitness Walker**
Walks several times a week for exercise. Wants scenic, quiet routes. Open to contributing reports if the flow is fast and frictionless.

**Linda — The Cautious Explorer**
Older walker prioritising traffic safety and infrastructure. Highly motivated to use an app but needs simplicity and accessibility features.

## 📌 Research Scope & Limitations

- Sample size is small (n=20); findings are directional, not statistically conclusive
- Respondents skewed toward the 30–45 age group (70%)
- Survey was self-reported — safety perceptions are subjective
- Geographic diversity was limited; one respondent noted a unique political context (Myanmar)
- Qualitative open-text responses were sparse; deeper interviews recommended as a next step

---

## 🔭 Suggested Next Steps

1. **Contextual interviews** with 5–8 participants from each persona group
2. **Usability testing** of the 3-tap reporting flow prototype
3. **Competitive analysis** of existing apps (Walkscore, Citymapper, SafeTrek)
4. **Quantitative validation** via a larger-scale survey (n=100+)
5. **Lighting data partnership** exploration with local councils or OpenStreetMap
